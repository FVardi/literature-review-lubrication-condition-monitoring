
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Lubrication of ball bearings is essential for reducing friction, wear, and overheating \cite{wakiru_review_2019,isalm_oil_2021}. Inadequate lubrication -- whether from starvation, viscosity degradation, or contamination -- increases asperity-to-asperity contact in the bearing, accelerates wear, raises operating temperature, and ultimately leads to premature bearing failure. Improper lubrication is estimated to cause between 30\% and 80\% of rolling element bearing failures \cite{jakobsen_detecting_2023,dai_situ_2024,xu_vibration-based_2024,nassef_impact_2022}. Despite this, lubrication condition monitoring (LCM) has received substantially less research attention than vibration-based fault detection and remaining useful life (RUL) estimation, which have been studied for decades and are now widely deployed in industry.

LCM of ball bearings encompasses the estimation of lubricant film thickness, lubricant viscosity and its adequacy for the operating conditions, lubricant degradation state (chemical and rheological aging), and contamination by wear debris or environmental ingress. These quantities collectively determine the ability of the lubricant to reduce friction and wear in the bearing contact zone. Accurate, online LCM would enable condition-based relubrication -- applying lubricant at the right time in the right quantity -- replacing time-based relubrication schedules that lead systematically to both under- and over-lubrication \cite{wakiru_review_2019}.

\subsection{Key Definitions}
\label{subsec:definitions}

Several terms require precise definition to clarify the distinctions between different sensing and signal processing approaches. This aids the definition of the scope of this review in relation to the existing literature. The following definitions are used throughout this review:

\subsubsection{Terminology for Sensing Methods}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Active vs.\ passive sensing:} A sensing method is \textit{active} if it supplies external energy into the bearing system in order to probe its response -- for example, by transmitting an ultrasonic pulse or applying an electrical current through the bearing. A sensing method is \textit{passive} if it relies solely on signals spontaneously generated by the bearing under operation -- for example, elastic stress waves from asperity contacts detected by an acoustic emission sensor.

    \item \textbf{Invasive vs.\ non-invasive sensing:} A sensing method is \textit{invasive} if it requires unobstructed line-of-sight to the lubricant, direct physical contact with the lubricant, mounting of sensors inside the bearing, structural modifications to the bearing or its housing, or physical extraction of lubricant samples. A sensing method is \textit{non-invasive} if none of these requirements apply.

    \item \textbf{Online, at-line, in-line, and offline monitoring.} \textit{Online} monitoring acquires data continuously during bearing operation without interruption or sample extraction. \textit{In-line} monitoring uses sensors integrated into the process stream (e.g., in the lubricant circulation system) and acquires data during operation. \textit{At-line} monitoring is performed near the process on samples taken from it but requires a brief interruption. \textit{Offline} monitoring involves laboratory analysis of extracted samples. This review focuses on online monitoring; in-line methods that do not require bearing stoppage are also included.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Terminology for Lubrication Conditions}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Lubrication film thickness} ($h_m$): the minimum thickness of the lubricant film in the elastohydrodynamic contact zone, calculated from the Hamrock--Dowson or similar models.
    \item \textbf{Specific film thickness} ($\Lambda$): the ratio of $h_m$ to the composite surface roughness of the bearing components, used to assign the lubrication regime.
    \item \textbf{Viscosity ratio} ($\kappa$): the ratio of the operating lubricant viscosity to the reference minimum viscosity required, as defined in ISO 281.
\end{itemize}

The quantities $h_m$, $\Lambda$, and $\kappa$ are the primary physics-based reference variables against which sensor-derived LCM features are calibrated and are explained in more detail in Section~\ref{sec:theory}.

\subsection{Monitoring Flow}
\label{subsec:flow}
An LCM program with clear sequential steps is defined in \cite{wakiru_review_2019}. This, however, considers actual sampling and direct analysis of the lubrication. We define the following steps for online LCM which relies on information gathering during bearing operation:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Sensing}: observation and data collection from a bearing in operation through sensors.
    \item \textbf{Signal processing}: signal processing and machine learning, which prepares the data for the downstream task of lubrication condition assessment. This step might contain dimensionality reduction through feature extraction or it might preserve the time-series structure of the data.
    \item \textbf{Lubrication condition assessment}: classification or regression on the processed data to infer the current lubrication condition in terms of lubrication regime, film thickness, degradation state or contamination; elaborated in Section~\ref{sec:theory}.
    \item \textbf{Maintenance decision}: a concrete relubrication strategy based on the lubrication condition assessment -- when and how much to lubricate to maintain or restore adequate lubrication level.
\end{enumerate}
The steps in the LCM-based maintenance pipeline are visualised in Figure~\ref{fig:lcm_flow}. LCM might be concerned with all steps 1--4 or just steps 1--3 with step 4 as a downstream task. Step 1--3 which include signal processing and lubrication condition assessment -- sitting between the raw sensor data and the maintenance decision -- are the core focus of this review.

\subsection{Existing Reviews and Their Limitations}
\label{subsec:existing_reviews}
A number of reviews address related topics in LCM. Wakiru et al.\ \cite{wakiru_review_2019} review LCM for maintenance decision support, covering direct lubricant analysis by invasive methods. Shang et al.\ \cite{shang_comprehensive_2024} review film thickness estimation by optical, electrical, and ultrasonic methods (active and/or invasive). Wang et al.\ \cite{wang_advanced_2024} review bearing condition monitoring including lubricant film thickness and wear debris detection, covering primarily active or invasive methods while mentioning passive ultrasound and acoustic emission measurements for condition monitoring. Hansen et al.\ \cite{hansen_comparison_2022} review calibration for ultrasonic reflectometry (active). Cen et al.\ \cite{cen_ehl_2021} and Becker-Dombrowsky et al.\ \cite{becker-dombrowsky_electrical_2024} review electrical capacitance and impedance methods (active). Dai et al.\ \cite{dai_situ_2024} review in-situ film thickness measurement by capacitance (active). Zhu et al.\ \cite{zhu_lubricating_2017,zhu_survey_2013} review online lubricant sensors and oil monitoring methods (active and invasive). Sun et al.\ \cite{sun_online_2021} review debris monitoring methods (active). Dou et al.\ \cite{dou_review_2022} review ultrasonic reflectometry for film thickness (active).

Three limitations are shared by all existing reviews. First, none provides a systematic taxonomy of signal processing methods across sensing modalities; signal processing is treated as a secondary detail beneath the sensing method taxonomy. Second, existing reviews overwhelmingly focus on active or invasive methods, leaving passive non-invasive methods -- which offer the greatest practical advantages for industrial deployment -- underrepresented. Third, no review identifies signal processing as the primary bottleneck limiting the advancement of LCM from controlled laboratory demonstrations to industrial deployment.

\subsection{Contribution and Scope of This Review}
\label{subsec:contribution}

This review provides the following contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{A systematic taxonomy of signal processing methods for LCM}, organised by SP method family and applied across all sensing modalities -- the first such taxonomy in the LCM literature.
    \item \textbf{A comprehensive comparative analysis} of sensing and SP method combinations across four dimensions: lubrication fault type sensitivity, operating condition sensitivity, deployment requirements, and technology maturity.
    \item \textbf{A structured identification of SP-centric research gaps} that limit the advancement of LCM to industrial practice, with specific future research directions for each gap.
    \item \textbf{Coverage of all sensing modalities} -- active and passive, invasive and non-invasive -- enabling a complete picture of the field, with greatest depth given to the passive non-invasive methods that are most relevant to industrial retrofit applications.
\end{enumerate}

This review covers the literature on online and in-line LCM of rolling element bearings (primarily ball bearings), with a focus on studies that describe signal processing methods in sufficient detail to assess their assumptions, limitations, and applicability. Studies concerned exclusively with bearing fault detection without a lubrication-specific focus, and studies on journal bearings or non-bearing tribological contacts, are excluded. The search covers literature up to early 2026.
